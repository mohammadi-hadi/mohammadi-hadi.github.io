Explainability-Based Token Replacement on LLM-Generated Text: Enhancing AI-Generated Text Undetectability

Hadi Mohammadi, Anastasia Giachanou, Daniel Oberski, Ayoub Bagheri

Utrecht University, The Netherlands

Paper Code arXiv

Abstract

The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has raised significant concerns about the detectability of AI-generated content. Current detection systems rely on identifying specific linguistic patterns and statistical signatures that distinguish machine-generated text from human writing. This paper explores how explainability techniques can be leveraged to understand and strategically modify these detectable patterns.

We propose a novel approach that uses explainability methods (LIME and SHAP) to identify which tokens most strongly signal AI authorship to detection systems. By selectively replacing these high-signal tokens with semantically equivalent alternatives, we demonstrate that detection accuracy can be significantly reduced while preserving the original meaning and quality of the text.

Our experiments show that this explainability-guided approach reduces detection rates by up to 45% while maintaining 92% semantic similarity with the original text. These findings have important implications for understanding the robustness of current detection methods and highlight the ongoing arms race between AI text generation and detection.

Key Contributions

  • XAI-Guided Token Identification: Novel use of explainability techniques to identify tokens most indicative of AI authorship
  • Semantic-Preserving Replacement: Token replacement strategies that maintain meaning while reducing detectability
  • Comprehensive Evaluation: Testing across multiple state-of-the-art AI text detectors
  • Robustness Analysis: Insights into weaknesses of current detection approaches

Methodology

Our approach consists of three main phases: detection signature analysis, token importance ranking, and strategic replacement. We leverage the interpretability of XAI methods to understand what makes text appear machine-generated.

1

Detection Analysis

Run AI-generated text through multiple detectors and collect prediction confidence scores for baseline assessment.

2

Token Attribution

Apply LIME and SHAP to identify which tokens contribute most to AI classification, creating an importance ranking.

3

Candidate Generation

Generate semantically equivalent replacement candidates using masked language models and synonym databases.

4

Strategic Replacement

Replace high-importance tokens while validating semantic preservation through embedding similarity.

Replacement Strategies

We evaluate multiple token replacement strategies, each with different trade-offs between detection evasion and semantic preservation:

Synonym Substitution

Replace tokens with synonyms from WordNet, prioritizing words with similar contextual embeddings.

MLM Prediction

Use masked language models to predict contextually appropriate alternatives for flagged tokens.

Paraphrase Insertion

Replace phrases rather than individual tokens using neural paraphrase generation.

Results

Our explainability-guided approach demonstrates significant reduction in detection rates across all tested detectors while preserving text quality.

Key Findings

Consistent Vulnerability Patterns

Detectors consistently rely on specific linguistic markers: formal register, consistent sentence structure, and particular transitional phrases. These patterns are identifiable through XAI analysis.

Token Position Matters

Tokens at sentence beginnings and paragraph transitions carry disproportionate weight in detection decisions, suggesting detectors learn structural rather than semantic patterns.

Cross-Detector Generalization

Replacements optimized for one detector often transfer to others, indicating shared vulnerability patterns across different detection architectures.

Semantic Preservation Trade-offs

More aggressive replacement yields greater detection evasion but risks semantic drift. Our approach finds an optimal balance point.

Ethical Considerations

This research is conducted to understand and improve AI text detection systems, not to enable misuse. We advocate for responsible disclosure and have shared our findings with detector developers. Understanding detector vulnerabilities is essential for building more robust detection methods.
